I was at Techoramaa developer conference focusing on .NET, Azure and this little thing called AI.
It's 2 days of educative, informative and sometimes inspiring sessions to help you become better at your job.
Those things are already really cool. But you can't follow 8 sessions straight in a row.
So there are breaks between each session and also ofcourse a lunchbreak as well.
During these breaks, you can socialize, mingle, network with other people and visit booths. There were a lot of companies promoting themselves and also trying to recruit. But they also provided mini challenges that people could do. When you win you could receive prices. These could be a PS5, Nintendo Switch 2 ( I want one so badly, I want to play Pokopia, plss), tech gadgets and ofcourse LEGO. You could also win tickets for a tombola raffle that happened on the end of the second day.

Today's blog is about one of those games and how I won a medieval LEGO set.

It's day 1 and I'm at the Team4Talent booth and I see 3 laptops. There are people lining up staring intensely at the screen, moving their mouse pondering about their next action.
I was curious so I went a bit closer and I found out it was a trivia challenge the trivia challenge included questionsabout.net basic stuff and some a bit more advanced stuff regarding usage of water tasks certain keywords how do you do declarations what are resources specific resources called in Azure I think one of the questions was about Azure Kubernetes service

Now the exact times and questions are vague in my head, but the goal is how I (with help from others) optimized each run[explain what a run is?] to get better results. [I need to make it visual so maybe mspaint or some other tool to visualize it?0

So the challenge consisted of multiple choice questions. Each correct answer awarded you point(s) and the goal is to get 9 points... as fast as possible. You could retry as many times as you like and only your top score and time combination counts. The top 3 would receive prices.

So I took my shot even though I was nervous having fake developer syndrome. But the questions started easy and went a bit higher up in difficulty later on.

I typed my name, clicked start and clicked the correct answer everytime (gambled on the one question about the team4talent speaker). I got in the first time at around a minute I think.
At the end of each run you could submit your name, mail and phone number and then you are added to the leaderboard. I saw people going sub 30 [explain what sub 30 is?] seconds. And as a competitive person I tried to improve my record.

1st optimization:
The first one is the easiest one (in terms of applying new mechanics). There are around 20-30 questions that are looped. So all I had to do was memorize the correct answer. The position of the answers are not fixed, so I had to actually read the choices first. By memorization I have shaved off a lot of time and got into sub 20 seconds. Which is a 40 second difference with my initial attempt.

2nd optimization:
Because the answers could be in different positions, I have positioned my mouse in between option 2 and option 3 [maybe clarify earlier in the explanation about how the challenge runs, that there are always 4 options]. So the mouse travel between the current position and the answer is minimal. This shaved off another second. But I knew we could go further, other people have started to notice as well. And tried to improve their times as well.

I tried something to optimize this as well but I had to rollback because it was doing more harm than good. I started wiggling the mouse while reading the question so I could move faster towards an option. This unfortunately did not work out and cost me more time than I would have liked. Sometimes you try things out and it doesn't work out. And that's ok.

3rd optimization:
Before each run, You have to fill in your name and then click Start to commence the run. The button is located at the bottom of the screen. The options were centered. Now I always clicked Start and then moved my mouse straight to option 2 and 3. I never thought about using the keyboard to my advantage. So the optimization was to fill in my name, move my mouse to the position between options 2 and 3 and then hit enter. This reduced travel time which gave me more time to scan the question and answers. This actually saved a lot of time and shaved an extra second off the record. We were already in sub 16 pace, but we knew we could improve even more.

4th optimization:
Now all of the previous optimizations were mechanically player improvements, but not about the game itself. Earlier in the blog I've mentioned you need 9 points. I did not write, answer 9 questions. There were answers that awarded 1 point, 2 points and 3 points (based on the difficulty of the questions). How could we use this to our advantage? A group of speedrunners ( yes we're at this point that we call ourselves speedrunners) were discussing strategies and trying to figure out how the point system works. Because if you could answer 3 3point questions correctly, you are faster than answering 9 1 point questions correctly. So how could we trigger the 3 point questions? I thought the question difficulty was random at the beginning, but the crew and I figured out that the easy questions came first, then the 2 point questions and then the 3 points questions. I think it always went like this:

- 3 1 point questions
- 3 2 point questions
- 3 3 point questions

So that made us think. We can't change the order of the questions, so how can we get to 9 points the quickest? How do we get to 9 points with the least amount of questions answered. Then we started to introduce failing answers. Now in a normal playthrough you always want to have everything correct. But in the speedrunning community you always got to go fast. We first started by deliberately failing 6 questions and then answering the 3 point questions correctly. This took a lot of time because each question answered took some time before proceeding to the next question. After some more testing we have figured out this sequence:

1. Answer 1 point question incorrectly (0)
2. Answer 1 point question incorrectly (0)
3. Answer 1 point question correctly (1)
4. Answer 2 point question correctly (3)
5. Answer 2 point question correctly (5)
6. Answer 2 point question correctly (7)
7. Answer 3 point question correctly (10)

Now why was this faster? Why not just answer the questions correctly since you already have to answer? Clicking a random answer is faster than reading the question and answering it. Let me explain. We did not care for the first three questions. We just needed to get at least 1 point from the first three questions as fast as possible, so I always selected the second option. (there are other optimizations that I see now while writing this blog, but we'll address those in the end, I am just following my train of thought at the moment this happened)
This was our first ( or second) breakthrough in understanding the game mechanics. This strat [explain strat? is it a gaming term?] allowed us to go sub 15 consistently. Now sometimes you answer the first two questions correctly, because of a misclick (imagine that, trying to fail a mission but succeeding, we truely are suffering from succes)

5th optimization:
This is another user input optimization. Because we knew the first two questions did not matter we needed to go through them as fast as possible. That's when Jurgen came in with another tactic. So after inputting your name and moving your mouse between option 2 and 3. you go

1. Enter (to start the first question)
2. Tab, space (to select the first answer of the first question)
3. Tab, space (to select the first answer of the second question)

This guaranteed us the quickest gains regardless of answer output, then based if you have 0 answers correctly, you could answer the third one correctly). Less brain overhead, more reactive measurements. This put us in sub 14 seconds. We knew that sub 13 was possible. But for now we just tried to get quicker at the mechanics we got.

6th and final optimization:
Now it is just a matter of reacting to the keywords and selecting the proper answer after the first 3 questions. The last optimization thaught us that by entering tab space we could always select the first answer. So I trained myself to ignore the 4th option. Why would I do that?

If in my head I remember the following flowchart for answers, I could react quicker.
[maybe add a mermaid?]
If answer is 1 ( go tab space), if answer is 2 (move mouse a bit up) if answer is 3 (move mouse a bit down). This reduced the most travel time for answering a question. I could answer the 4th question but it would have affected my speed. This doesn't mean that sometimes I did not select the fourth option out of muscle memory. This put me in sub 13 seconds.

Now after learning, discussing, testing and applying all these tactics I finally reached my magnus opus sub 12 seconds. I can't remember the exact time but it was 11,3... seconds. One thing that all these optimizations could not predict was luck. I had a lot of luck with that run that I could blaze through all the questions. I felt satisfied and extremely happy that I was at the top of the leaderboard. And by this I managed to win this beautiful lego set. And even though at the end of the second day of the conference I was content with lego in my hands, friends and colleagues around me, I still could not help but think, what if sub 11 was possible...
